The Long
Way Around

A career that began on the shop floor, passed through corporate engineering, and found its way back — on purpose.

Written by
Chris Khamken
Read time
~12 min
Filed under
About · Career

Some people find their calling in a classroom. Others find it in a moment of inspiration, a mentor's advice, or a chance encounter that changes everything. For me, it happened somewhere between the hum of a CNC spindle and the smell of cutting fluid on aluminum — before I even knew what a career was.

My father ran a machine shop. And long before I understood tolerances, tool paths, or the difference between a roughing pass and a finishing pass, I understood this: something happens when raw material meets a sharp edge and a steady hand. Something becomes something else. That, it turns out, is enough to build a life around.

J&P Khamken Industries — co-founded and operated by my father and his brother starting in 2000 — didn't start as a defense contractor. It started as a commercial machine shop, renting warehouse space, building a reputation the old-fashioned way: show up, make quality parts, deliver on time. What makes that story worth telling is the context behind it: my father and his family fled to the United States as refugees during the Vietnam War, arriving with little and building everything from scratch. Starting a machine shop from the ground up, in a country that wasn't the one they were born into, and growing it into a nationally recognized defense manufacturer — that's the American dream in the most literal sense.

More than 1,000 contracts. 100% on-time or early delivery record. AS9100 aerospace quality certification. Subcontract work for Lockheed Martin, Raytheon Missile Systems, BAE, General Dynamics, and L-3 Communications. In 2011, J&P Khamken Industries was awarded the SBA Region IV Subcontractor of the Year, the National Subcontractor of the Year, and the Defense Logistics Agency Vendor Excellence Award for Small Disadvantaged Business.

I was part of that story in the early years, working part-time at the shop while still in school. By the time I graduated from Auburn in 2010, the business my dad and uncle had built from a rented warehouse into an award-winning aerospace and defense manufacturer was operating at a level few small shops ever reach. That context never left me. The combination of craft, quality systems, and sheer reliability that made J&P successful quietly became the standard I've held my own work to ever since.

Starting from scratch — literally

I didn't start in the office. I started at the machine.

Growing up with the shop meant that my education in manufacturing started long before any classroom. Through high school and into college, I worked there part-time — progressing from basic equipment to waterjet to full CNC operation. Before I ever opened a CAD package professionally, I was reading and writing G-code by hand. Not because it was required, but because understanding why a machine moves the way it does made everything else make sense.

That foundation has never left me. Every design decision I've made since — every tolerance I've called out, every fixturing approach I've chosen — has been filtered through the question a machinist always asks: can this actually be made? It's a question a lot of engineers forget to ask.

The pivot that changed everything

In high school, I was introduced to AutoCAD. Interesting enough. Then my high school engineering teacher — someone I consider a genuine mentor — put SolidWorks in front of me, and something clicked that hasn't unclicked since.

I remember spending hours in that software that I wasn't required to spend. Voluntarily. Evenings, weekends, whenever I could get in front of a screen. Not because anyone told me to, but because 3D modeling felt like the bridge between the ideas in my head and the parts I could hold in my hands. That gap — between concept and physical reality — became the space I wanted to live in professionally.

Around the same time, I joined our school's team for the B.E.S.T. Robotics Competition. Designing and building under pressure, with real constraints and a hard deadline, was the best early lesson in what engineering actually looks like outside a textbook.

Auburn, formula cars, and autonomous lawnmowers

At Auburn University, I pursued a Bachelor's in Mechanical Engineering — and made sure most of it happened outside the lecture hall.

I joined the Formula SAE team, where I helped fabricate the car frame and machined components that ended up going around a track at speed. There's a different kind of pride in watching something you made perform under load. Later, I moved to a collegiate IEEE robotics team, where we designed and built competition robots from scratch — cross-functional, high-pressure, real results.

My senior capstone was the kind of project you either love or lose sleep over: designing, building, and competing with an autonomous robotic lawnmower in the ION competition. We did all three. It was messy, iterative, and one of the most genuinely satisfying engineering experiences I've had.

Auburn gave me the degree. The teams gave me the education.

Five years in the trenches at CRH

After graduating, I briefly worked as a Process Engineer at J&P Khamken Industries — by then a nationally recognized defense contractor operating at the peak of what my dad and uncle had built over a decade. It was a grounding reminder of where the whole thing started, and a preview of what I'd eventually return to. But the pull toward something new was strong, and I accepted a position with CRH North America, a manufacturer specializing in automotive seat frames.

For over five years, I was embedded in the real machinery of product development: designing tool and die for sheet metal stamping and forming, and engineering the fixtures and stations that brought those parts together. Welding fixtures. Assembly fixtures. Semi-automated stations for orbital riveting, hydraulic riveting, tube swaging, burnishing. The full spectrum of how a seat frame goes from flat sheet stock to a finished structural assembly.

I also had the opportunity to travel — to Michigan and Tennessee for domestic projects, and internationally to Saltillo, Mexico and Świebodzin, Poland. There's a particular kind of professional development that only comes from sitting across a conference table from engineers who approach the same problem from an entirely different cultural and technical tradition. I came back from those trips a sharper thinker every time.

The first seed of something bigger

During my later years at CRH, I started Khamken Designs, LLC — quietly, on the side.

Contract design work. 3D printing services. A small but deliberate step toward the idea that I might eventually work for myself. It didn't last long in that form; the demands of full-time work made it impossible to give it what it needed. So I put it on the shelf. But I never stopped thinking about it.

Some ideas don't go away. They just wait.

Automation, Tesla, and thinking bigger

My next move took me to Teamtechnik in the north Atlanta metro — and opened up a much wider view of what automated manufacturing could look like.

Here, I was designing automated end-of-line test (EOLT) equipment for the automotive sector, as well as automated assembly and test systems for medical devices. The customer list got more interesting: among them was Tesla, for whom we designed equipment that was ultimately installed at the Reno Gigafactory. I also had the chance to work directly alongside engineers from Germany, which added another layer of technical rigor and a different philosophy around precision and process documentation.

It was at Teamtechnik where my understanding of automation matured from "how do we move this part" to "how do we build a system that is reliable, serviceable, and scalable."

Five projects, one year, one big education

From there, I joined a well-regarded Fanuc-authorized robotics integrator in Atlanta — a small, privately held, family-run operation with deep roots in the Georgia engineering community and strong ties to Georgia Tech. Walking in the door, I felt something I hadn't expected: it reminded me of my dad's shop. The culture, the ownership mentality, the way everyone had skin in the outcome.

In the span of about a year, I completed five large robotic cell projects as the mechanical project lead. Each one was its own world — food and beverage, foundry, building materials, automotive, metals. Different materials, different payloads, different environmental conditions, different customers with different expectations.

That year compressed more varied robotic integration experience into a shorter period than most engineers see in a decade. Commissioning and supporting installations at job sites in Los Angeles, New Mexico, Louisiana, South Carolina, Alabama, and the Atlanta metro. There's no substitute for standing on a production floor watching a cell you designed come to life, troubleshooting in real time with a customer looking over your shoulder.

Back to BBS Automation — and Rivian

I returned to my former employer — by then acquired and rebranded as BBS Automation — as a Senior Mechanical Design Engineer, stepping in as mechanical lead on multiple high-profile projects for Rivian — specifically production equipment for the R1 and R2 platforms.

If Tesla was the moment I understood the scale of what modern automotive manufacturing demanded, Rivian was the moment I understood the pace. EV production doesn't wait. Timelines are compressed, tolerances on program schedules are as tight as tolerances on parts, and the expectation is that the engineering team stays ahead of the build.

I did that work well. And somewhere in the middle of it, I started having a familiar feeling.

The decision

There's a version of this story where I keep climbing the engineering ladder — senior lead to principal, principal to director, title after title, further and further from the things that made me want to do this in the first place.

I chose a different version.

The draw back to a shop environment had never really left. Hands-on work. Problem-solving that starts with a drawing and ends with a finished part. The tangible satisfaction of making something real. I'd spent years designing systems that others built, and I was good at it — but I missed being the one with chips on my shoes.

In 2025, I fully committed. I relocated Khamken Designs, LLC from Montgomery, Alabama to Suwanee, Georgia, and went all-in — running CNC machining operations as a job shop alongside continuing mechanical design contract work. My father, who had come out of semi-retirement to help keep things moving in Montgomery, handed the baton back. It came full circle in the best possible way.

Where things stand now

Every day looks different, which is exactly how I want it.

Some days I'm deep in SolidWorks, working through a design challenge for a client. Other days I'm at the machine, dialing in a setup, checking a first article, talking through a job with a customer who needs something made that doesn't exist yet. The combination of engineering judgment and shop-floor capability is the whole point — it's what lets me take a project from concept to finished component without losing anything in translation.

I've thought occasionally about what would have happened if I'd taken over my dad's original shop years earlier, when the idea first occurred to me. Honestly, I'm glad I didn't. I needed the years at CRH, at Teamtechnik and BBS Automation, in the robotics world. I needed to see how large-scale manufacturing works from the inside, to understand automation at a systems level, to travel and collaborate and build a professional toolkit that most job shop owners don't have.

The timing is right now because I wasn't ready then.
