Laos:
The Country My Family Left Behind

Trip dates
February 2008
Duration

Context
Part of 3-week family roots trip

Read time

We crossed from Thailand into Laos the same way my family had left it, in reverse — over the Mekong. Except we used a bridge. My dad had used a different method, thirty years earlier, in the middle of the night, as a child.

Laos was the second leg of a three-week roots trip my dad and I took in February 2008, the February between my sophomore and junior years at Auburn. We'd spent the first week in Nakhon Phanom, Thailand, at my grandmother Soumaly's hometown on the Thai bank of the Mekong. Now we were crossing to the other side. My grandfather Loy's country. My dad's country. The place he hadn't been since he was a boy smart enough and restless enough to figure out how to leave it alive.

Two weeks. Savannakhet first, then north to Vientiane, with side trips to Thakhek, Vang Vieng, and the Nam Ngum reservoir. I didn't know what to expect. I'd grown up hearing this country talked about in the past tense — something that existed before, something left behind. Being there made it present tense for the first time.

Stop 01 · Savannakhet

My Grandfather's Town

Savannakhet is different from Nakhon Phanom in ways that are hard to quantify but immediately felt. Fewer paved roads. More red dirt. Quieter. The kind of town where you can tell nothing much has changed and nothing much is trying to. My grandfather Loy grew up here — military police, Royal Lao Government, a man whose rank and status is the reason his family was able to flee when the Pathet Lao took over after 1975. Former government officials and military personnel were the first targets of the new regime's re-education campaigns. The family knew what was coming.

My grandmother Soumaly and four of her children fled first. My grandfather followed with one of his siblings. My dad was last. He was a teenager, and he used what he had — street smarts, a habit of not staying put, and the kind of resourcefulness that comes from growing up somewhere that required it. He made it across the Mekong and didn't look back.

We stayed with my grandfather's sister — my dad's aunt — whose husband ran a Buddha statue workshop in the compound. Not as a hobby. As a life. The process started with concrete poured into molds, then workers would hand-finish each piece — adding the fine surface details and small ornamental elements that molds can't produce. Statues in various stages lined the courtyard walls: raw castings, half-finished pieces being worked on, completed figures waiting. The workshop was open to the air and always in motion. It was one of the more quietly remarkable things I've seen.

The uncle's workshop — concrete poured into molds, then hand-finished. Every stage of production in the same courtyard.

At some point during the week, my dad's aunt told us she had been saving the vacant lot next to her property. For us. For my dad's children — me and my sister. She wanted to pass it down.

It was one of the most generous things anyone has ever offered me and one of the easiest things I've ever helped turn down. My dad talked to her gently but plainly: the land would be worth more to her sold than sitting idle waiting for two people who lived on the other side of the world and had no idea what they'd do with it. She needed the money more than we needed the sentiment. She understood. The offer was entirely from the heart. The response had to be practical.

"She had been saving that land for decades. For us. My dad talked her out of it in an afternoon — not unkindly, just honestly."

Savannakhet — quiet streets, wats along the Mekong, the unhurried rhythm of a provincial town

One afternoon my dad took me on a walk through the neighborhood where he grew up. Chomkeo. The streets looked the same as they had when he left — same layout, same proportions, same kind of low wooden houses set back from the road. He pointed things out as we walked: where certain families had lived, where kids used to gather, the shortcuts he knew by heart.

Then we turned a corner and found the sign. B. Chomkeo. His neighborhood, still there, still marked. He stood next to it and I took the photo. He didn't say much. He didn't need to.

A few streets later, we passed a house and an old woman was sitting outside — the same house she'd apparently always lived in. She looked up. My dad stopped. They stared at each other for a moment across thirty years of distance, and then they both knew. She had helped look after him and the neighborhood kids when he was growing up. He had been gone for decades. She had never left. They talked for a long time.

"She had been sitting outside the same house for thirty years. He had been gone for all of them. They recognized each other immediately."

The neighborhood sign. The woman who remembered him. Two moments from the same afternoon walk.

His elementary school, still standing. The streets he ran as a kid — barely changed.

Stop 02 · The River, Again

The Other Bank

My dad's cousin had a restaurant on the Mekong. We ate there one evening — open air, right on the bank, Thailand visible across the water. A week earlier I had stood on the Thai side looking at Laos. Now I was on the Lao side looking at Thailand. Same river, opposite banks, the same wide brown water in between.

I thought about my grandfather swimming it. I thought about my dad crossing it as a teenager. The river looked exactly as peaceful as it had looked from Nakhon Phanom. Peace is not the right word for what it is, but it's what it looks like.

The Mekong · Savannakhet, looking west toward Thailand
Stop 03 · Thakhek

Caves and Karst

My dad's aunt took us to Thakhek for a day — about an hour north of Savannakhet, right on the Mekong. The karst limestone landscape there is dramatic in a way that photographs don't quite capture: massive formations rising straight up from flat ground, riddled with caves, the whole thing reflected in the river on calm mornings. It's the kind of scenery that makes you understand why people build temples here. The land itself seems deliberately arranged.

We visited a handful of wats and explored some of the cave systems in the area. One required descending a long staircase cut into the rock face — carved and maintained, lit throughout, a proper path. Standing inside and looking back toward the entrance, the opening framed a perfect rectangle of blue sky and jungle green. These landscapes have been sacred for a long time, and being inside the mountain you understand why.

Stop 04 · The Road North

Savannakhet to Vientiane

The drive north from Savannakhet to Vientiane takes most of a day. Route 13, the main north-south highway — flat in the south, the landscape opening up as you go. Occasional towns, roadside vendors, monks walking the shoulders of the road, tuk-tuks loaded with more than seems physically possible. The road is good now. My dad remembered it differently.

There's something about driving through a country with a parent who grew up there. He'd note things — a town name, a stretch of road, something that triggered a memory or a story. Some of it was geography. Some of it was history. The country looked peaceful from the window of a car in 2008. That wasn't always what it was.

The road north — a day's drive through the flat Lao interior

Stop 05 · Nam Ngum Reservoir

The Islands

A day trip from Vientiane took us to Nam Ngum — a reservoir about 90 kilometers north, formed when the Nam Ngum River was dammed in 1971. We hired a private boat for a few hours, a long-tail that took us slowly between the islands scattered across the water. The reservoir is wide and still, the islands rising from it in varying sizes, some forested, some bare red earth.

The guide mentioned, almost in passing, that some of the islands had been used as re-education camps after 1975 — remote enough to be inescapable, isolated enough to be forgotten. The official term was "seminar camps." People sent there were often never heard from again. Former military officers. Government officials. Teachers. Anyone who had been associated with the previous order. My grandfather's type of person.

I watched the islands slide past from the boat. Water buffalos swam between two of them, unhurried, heads above the waterline. The sky was clear. The water was flat. It was a beautiful day on a reservoir with a quiet history, and the boat kept moving.

"The islands looked peaceful. The guide explained what they had been used for after 1975. I watched them slide past and didn't say much."

Nam Ngum Reservoir — lunch on a private boat, the islands quiet in the afternoon light

Stop 06 · Vang Vieng

The Tourist Town

Vang Vieng was a different kind of stop. My dad's cousin Kot came with us — a younger cousin, more our generation, easier to move around with. The town sits in a valley ringed by the same karst formations as Thakhek, with the Nam Song River running through it. In 2008 it was still relatively small — backpacker bars, tubing, a loose traveler vibe. I've seen photos of what it looks like now. It's grown into something else entirely.

We spent a day or two on the water — tubing, swimming, the things you do in a place built for it. And we explored two caves in the hills above town.

The first one required a hike. Real rough-terrain hiking, through brush and foliage up the hillside, no path to speak of. When we got to the entrance it was dark inside — recently discovered, no infrastructure, no lights. We went in with handheld flashlights and didn't venture far. The darkness past the beam of the flashlight was total. The cave had formations that hadn't been documented yet. Being among the first people to see something like that is a strange feeling — the mountain has been there for millions of years and you are temporarily inside it with a flashlight.

The second cave was Tham Chang. This one had been developed — a tall staircase cut into the rock to reach the entrance, lights strung throughout, a designated walking path. The formations inside were extraordinary, properly lit, accessible. Both caves were worthwhile. The undiscovered one was more memorable.

Two caves — one undiscovered with flashlights only, one developed with lights and a staircase

Stop 07 · Vientiane

The Capital

Vientiane is a capital city that doesn't perform like one. It's small, unhurried, the streets wide and mostly quiet, the Mekong running along its western edge. We stayed with my dad's aunt and uncle — his side of the family — and the week was layered: family dinners, sightseeing, and toward the end, a wedding reception.

One evening we drove across town to have dinner with one of my mom's cousins. Home-cooked, a full table, the particular warmth of people who are glad you made the effort to come. My mom's family is from Vientiane and I had never met most of them. The trip had been arranged as my dad's roots journey but my mom's roots were here too, and this dinner was the bridge to that side.

Dinner with my mom's cousins · a home-cooked meal across town — the first time meeting this side of the family

We visited Pha That Luang — the great golden stupa that is the national symbol of Laos, the most recognizable structure in the country. Patuxai, the victory gate modeled on the Arc de Triomphe but built with Lao ornamentation. From the top of Patuxai you can see the whole main boulevard stretching south, the city laid out flat in every direction, the Mekong a smear of silver at the edge of the view.

Pha That Luang · the national symbol of Laos, Vientiane

Vientiane landmarks — Pha That Luang, Patuxai, the city from above

The wedding reception for my dad's cousin Khao was the last major event of the trip. Large, traditional, the kind of Lao ceremony that goes until late — food, music, family from multiple directions all in one place. I didn't participate in the rituals but I helped celebrate. There's a particular feeling of being at a family celebration in a country your family left, watching cousins and aunts and uncles gathered together, and understanding that this is what the other branch of the family tree looks like. The branch that stayed.

The daytime baci ceremony at the house — traditional silk, marigold garlands, elders tying white strings of blessing on the couple's wrists

The baci — one of the most sacred Lao rituals. Elders tie white cotton strings on the wrists of the couple, each string a blessing.

Khao's wedding reception · hotel ballroom, Vientiane

Evening reception at the hotel, then late into the night back at the house — the celebration that kept going

On the last morning I thought about the shape of the three weeks. Nakhon Phanom: my grandmother Soumaly's hometown, the Mekong from the Thai bank, the place the family came to first. Savannakhet: my grandfather Loy's town, the river from the Lao bank, the neighborhood sign, the woman who remembered my dad as a boy. Vientiane: the capital, the golden stupa, the cousins who had grown up here while my family had grown up in Alabama.

My grandfather Loy served the old government and that made him a target when the new one came. His status and connections allowed the family to leave — his rank, whatever was left of his authority in the final days before everything changed. He got the family out. My dad got himself out. The family ended up in Dadeville, Alabama, sponsored by a family called the Olivers, and rebuilt everything from nothing.

Standing in the country they left, I kept thinking about what it cost. Not money. The other kind of cost. The Chomkeo neighborhood sign. The aunt's vacant lot. The old woman outside her house. The re-education islands sliding past in the afternoon light.

Laos is a beautiful country. That's a strange thing to say about a place your family had to flee. It's also just true.

About Loy

My grandfather served as military police under the Royal Lao Government. When the Pathet Lao took power in 1975, former officials and military personnel were primary targets for re-education. His status and connections allowed the family to flee — my grandmother first with four children, then my grandfather, then my dad last, using the resourcefulness he had spent a childhood developing. The family reunited at a refugee camp in Thailand before immigrating to Dadeville, Alabama.
