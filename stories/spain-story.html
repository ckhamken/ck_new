Introduction
The trip that started everything

I heard stories told by friends who had traveled internationally before and it was enough to get me curious about embarking on some travels myself. Spain was my first real opportunity — a Spanish III class trip organized through EF Tours, coordinated around a week-and-a-half of sightseeing beginning in Madrid before shuttling south through Toledo, Córdoba, Seville, and the Costa del Sol, then east to Granada, and back north to Segovia before returning to Madrid.

I didn't know it at the time, but fourteen days in Spain at nineteen years old would set a pattern I'd follow for the next two decades. There's something about the first time you land somewhere where you don't entirely know the language, don't recognize the food on the menu, and have no instinct for how things work — that disorientation is the point. It forces you to pay attention.

"The disorientation is the point. It forces you to pay attention."
Stop 01 · Capital
MadridGateway to Spain
2 days · Arrival, Royal Palace, Paseo del Prado, Las Ventas

We landed in Madrid in the morning and took a chartered bus to check into our hotel. The rest of that first day was deliberately unscheduled — exchange money, eat something, fight off the jet lag. Madrid had other ideas. Even exhausted from the overnight flight, the city had an energy that made it hard to stay inside.

The next morning we walked out to find the Paseo del Prado lined with flowering trees and Madrileños going about their morning routines — sitting on benches, walking dogs, moving at a pace that felt entirely unbothered by whatever the rest of the world was doing. The Royal Palace came next, massive and impossibly ornate, its chapel interior so covered in gilded altar pieces and Baroque frescoes that every room felt excessive and overwhelming in the best possible way.

On the way through the city we also passed the Plaza de Toros de Las Ventas — Madrid's famous bullring, its Moorish-style brick facade absolutely unmistakable. We didn't go in, but it's the kind of building that stops you on a sidewalk regardless.

Stop 02 · Day trip from Madrid
ToledoCity of Three Cultures
1 day · Historic old city, El Greco country

Toledo sits on a granite hill surrounded on three sides by the Tagus river, about an hour south of Madrid by bus. For most of its history it was a city where Christians, Muslims, and Jews lived and worked alongside each other — a fact you feel in the architecture, where a single block might show a mosque turned cathedral turned something else entirely.

Our stop included lunch at what felt like a traditional Castilian farmstead — a patio restaurant under a reed canopy, old wagon wheels and terracotta pots, the kind of place you'd drive past a hundred times without noticing. One of those accidental stops that ends up being more memorable than the monuments it sits between.

Stop 03 · Andalusia
CórdobaWhere civilizations met
1–2 days · La Mezquita, Judería, flamenco in the streets

Córdoba sits in the heart of Andalusia, and it hits you differently than Madrid or Toledo. The air is warmer, the light harder, the streets narrower. The Mezquita — a mosque converted to a cathedral in the 13th century — is one of those places that's genuinely hard to explain. You walk through eight hundred arches of alternating red and white stone expecting a museum experience and instead feel something older and harder to categorize.

Outside, the fortified perimeter wall runs for hundreds of meters along the street, its crenellated battlements lined with orange trees — the contrast between stone and green canopy is something I keep coming back to in memory. The tower rising above the mosque's corner is a later Baroque addition to what was originally a minaret, layered centuries visible in one structure.

Then we turned a corner and stumbled into a group of women in full flamenco dress — polka dot ruffles, fans, castanets — dancing in the middle of the street as if nobody had asked and nobody needed to. A local festival, completely unrelated to the tourist circuit. Nobody performing for us. Just Spain doing what it does.

"Nobody performing for us. Just Spain doing what it does."

Stop 04 · Andalusia
SevilleThe most Spanish city in Spain
2 days · Cathedral & Giralda, Real Alcázar, Itálica, Plaza de España

Seville moves differently from every other city on this trip. There's a confidence to it — an understanding that this is where flamenco was born, where Columbus launched from, where the oranges grow in the street medians and nobody bothers picking them. The Cathedral of Seville is the largest Gothic building in the world, and standing at street level next to the old Alcázar walls, it doesn't let you forget it.

La Giralda — originally a Moorish minaret, later topped with a Renaissance bell tower — rises above everything around it with a kind of quiet authority. We climbed it. Not stairs but a continuous ramp, wide enough for horses to have carried royalty up in its original life. At the top, the bronze bells hang at eye level in open Moorish arches, the brick and ironwork close enough to touch. Below them, the whole city of Seville spreads out flat in every direction — the Plaza de la Maestranza bullring visible in the middle distance, the Cathedral rooftop spread just beneath you, the Guadalquivir river catching light somewhere beyond.

Inside the Cathedral itself, the scale takes a minute to register: the painted vault above you is the size of a warehouse, covered in gilded angels and baroque figures that look small only because they're so far up. Gothic architecture at this scale stops being architecture and starts being something closer to geology — you're inside it the way you're inside a mountain.

The Plaza de España was still being staged for something when we arrived — equipment, barriers, some kind of event setup scattered across the tile-inlaid esplanade. Even in partial disarray it was enormous and strangely beautiful.

Side trip · Itálica Roman Ruins
Just outside Seville lies Itálica — one of the first Roman cities established in the Iberian Peninsula, founded around 206 BC. Its amphitheater is among the largest in the Roman world, capable of holding 25,000 spectators. Walking the arena floor, looking up at the eroded seating banks, the hypogeum tunnels still intact below your feet — it's the kind of place that recalibrates your sense of how old things can actually be.

Stop 05 · Andalusia
Costa del SolThe white villages
2 days · Mijas, Mediterranean coast, pueblos blancos

The Costa del Sol section of the trip took us through the pueblos blancos — the white villages of Andalusia — most notably Mijas, which clings to the hillside above the coast with a complete indifference to vertical construction logic. Every building is whitewashed. The streets are too narrow for cars. Flower boxes hang from iron railings on every façade.

From the upper vantage points you can see out over the whole coastline — the Mediterranean hazy and blue in the distance, the newer resort towns spreading along the shore, and above them the old village sitting completely unchanged. Calle Málaga — just one of dozens of narrow lanes threading through the village — looked exactly like it probably looked a century earlier.

Stop 06 · Granada
The AlhambraNothing quite prepares you
2 days · Nasrid Palaces, Generalife gardens, views over Granada

Every trip needs one thing that exceeds expectations, and the Alhambra was it. It sits above Granada on a forested hill, a fortress-palace complex that the Nasrid dynasty built and refined over two centuries, then abandoned in 1492 when Ferdinand and Isabella completed the Reconquista. The last Moorish sultan supposedly wept when he looked back at it for the final time. Looking at it from the Generalife gardens across the valley, you understand why.

The Patio de los Arrayanes — the Court of the Myrtles — is the image most people associate with the Alhambra: a long rectangular pool reflecting the arcaded palace facade perfectly. What photos don't show is the stillness of it, even surrounded by other visitors. It feels like something designed to slow you down. Every surface is covered in geometric tilework or carved stucco or calligraphy. The Moorish builders seemingly couldn't leave anything undecorated, and somehow it never feels excessive.

The Generalife — the summer palace gardens above the main complex — is a quieter counterpoint. Water channels run through hedged walkways, fountains catch the light at odd angles, and from the highest terraces you can look back across the valley and see the whole Alhambra spread below you, its towers and walls embedded in the hillside like they grew there.

"Every surface is covered. There's no blank wall anywhere. Somehow it never feels excessive."

Stop 07 · Return north
Segovia& back to Madrid
1 day · Roman aqueduct, Alcázar, final night in Madrid

The last stop before returning to Madrid was Segovia — a smaller city about an hour northwest of the capital, famous for its Roman aqueduct and its Alcázar, the castle that may have inspired Disney's Cinderella Castle. After two weeks of Moorish palaces and baroque cathedrals, there's something grounding about standing in front of a 2,000-year-old Roman engineering project that's still intact, still spanning the same valley, still technically functional.

Fourteen days is enough to see a lot of Spain and not enough to understand any of it. The camera ran out of film somewhere before Segovia — a very 2004 problem, back when you bought a Kodak disposable and hoped the roll lasted the trip. Some things just don't get photographed. They stay where they belong: in memory, approximate and a little warmer than they probably were.

I've been back to thinking about Spain since — the pacing of Andalusian life, the density of history in a single city block, the food, the light in the afternoon. The trip that starts curiosity is rarely the one that satisfies it.

"The trip that starts curiosity is rarely the one that satisfies it."
