A Week in Thailand:
Origins of My Grandmother

Trip dates
February 2008
Duration
1 week
Base
Nakhon Phanom
Context
Part of 3-week family roots trip
Photos
103
Read time
11 min

I was twenty, burnt out, and running on empty by the end of sophomore year. The kind of tired that sleep doesn't fix — the kind that comes from two years of grinding through engineering coursework without ever stopping to ask why. My dad had a different prescription: come with me to Southeast Asia for three weeks. We're going to where we're from.

I said yes before he finished the sentence.

The trip was structured as a roots journey — Nakhon Phanom, Thailand first, where my grandmother Soumaly grew up. Then east across the Mekong into Laos: Savannakhet, where my grandfather's family lived and where my dad spent his childhood. Then Vientiane, where my mom's side is from. Three countries, three weeks, one thread connecting all of it: the story of how my family ended up in Alabama, sponsored by a family from Dadeville, by way of a war and a refugee camp and a lot of people who were brave enough to start over.

Thailand was the beginning. Gate 43 at Suvarnabhumi, a flight number, a destination on a board: Bangkok. Something I'd only ever known through the smell of my grandmother's kitchen and half-remembered stories was about to become a real place I could stand in.

Suvarnabhumi Airport, Bangkok · arrival from the US

Stop 01 · Nakhon Phanom

My Grandmother's Town

Nakhon Phanom sits on the eastern edge of Thailand's Isan region, pressed right up against the Mekong. On the far bank, Laos. The town is quiet in the way that only provincial towns in Southeast Asia can be — not sleepy, just unhurried. Life moves at a pace that doesn't require justification. My great-aunt's house was about two blocks from the river, and within an hour of arriving I understood that this would be the rhythm of the week: eat, talk, walk to the river, repeat.

The relatives we stayed with were my grandmother Soumaly's siblings and their families — people my dad hadn't seen since he immigrated to the United States during the Vietnam War. Decades had passed. The reunions happened at kitchen tables and in shaded courtyards, in a language I don't speak but fully understand. I sat at the edges of those conversations and watched my dad become someone I hadn't quite seen before. Looser. More at ease. Parts of his past returning to him gradually, like a language coming back after years of not using it.

I kept noticing things. A gesture from a great-uncle that I recognized as my dad's. The way a cousin laughed — exactly like my mom. Small physical echoes everywhere I looked, a quiet proof that these strangers and I came from the same place. I'd grown up with my grandmother pouring her love onto me in a very specific way — present, patient, unconditional. Sitting in that house in Nakhon Phanom, surrounded by her siblings doing the same thing, I realized it wasn't a personal quirk. It was how that family loved. She had carried that all the way to Alabama.

"It was my first time being there, but it felt like home — because I could feel the same love being poured onto me that my grandmother had always given."

Nakhon Phanom · daily life around the house

Some mornings my dad and I would walk down to the Mekong with one of his uncles after breakfast. There wasn't much to do there besides stand and look. The river is wide — wider than you expect — and slow, and the color of milky tea. Laos is right there on the other side, green and low and close enough that you could practically call across.

One morning my dad mentioned, almost in passing, that he had swum across this river when he fled Laos. I stopped walking. The water looked calm from where I was standing. Peaceful, even. I stared at the far bank for a long time after that.

"My dad swam across that river. I was standing on a riverbank looking at the place he had escaped from."

Stop 02 · The River

The Mekong

The Mekong isn't just a river here. It's a border, a memory, a dividing line between the life my family had and the one they built. Standing on the Thai bank, you're looking at Laos. And if you're the child of someone who left Laos with nothing, you're looking at everything they left behind.

Farmers worked the exposed river gardens in the dry season — families out in the morning, tending crops on the sandbanks and floodplains that the river reveals when the water drops. Dogs wandered the bank. Longtail boats moved slowly upstream. Papaya trees grew heavy with fruit in the haze. It was one of the most peaceful scenes I've ever been in the middle of, and I kept thinking about what it must have looked like to someone swimming across it at night, not knowing if they'd make it.

Stop 03 · The Festival

Wat Phra That Phanom

We happened to arrive during the annual Phra That Phanom Festival — one of the most important Buddhist pilgrimages in the Isan region, drawing tens of thousands of people from across northeastern Thailand and Laos. The timing was pure luck, or maybe not. The wat is sacred to the Thai-Lao people of this part of the world in a way that's hard to overstate. For a week every year, the region comes home to it.

I was not prepared for what it looked like. The stupa itself — a white and gold prang that rises above everything else in Nakhon Phanom — was surrounded by monks in saffron, by families making offerings, by processions with ceremonial baskets and golden decorations stacked impossibly high. The sound of gongs. The smell of incense and grilled food from the market stalls that lined every street leading to the wat. Elephants moving slowly through the crowd. It was sensory overload in the best possible way — nothing curated, nothing performed for tourists, just thousands of people doing something that mattered to them.

I didn't share the religious practice, but I understood the pull of it. This was a place where people came to honor what came before them. That felt relevant.

Phra That Phanom Festival · tens of thousands gather annually

We walked through the grounds for most of the day. My dad's relatives knew where to go, what to see, what the different rituals meant. I followed and watched and photographed. The monks seated in rows near the old chedis. A woman striking a gong the size of a dining table. Processions of young people in traditional dress carrying offerings. Market vendors selling pha sin fabric, woven baskets, and food I didn't recognize but ate anyway.

Late in the afternoon the light went golden and the crowds thickened around the stupa. Everyone moving slowly, reverently. My dad stood quietly near one of the outer gates for a long time, watching. I didn't interrupt whatever he was thinking about.

Wat Phra That Phanom Festival — monks, processions, markets, and thousands of pilgrims

Elephants moving through the festival grounds — one of many things I had no frame of reference for

Stop 04 · Around the Table

The Meals

If the Mekong was the emotional center of the trip, the meals were the social one. Every day involved food — preparing it, eating it, talking over it for hours after. My great-aunt's kitchen was small and always in use. The communal dinners happened around tables covered in dishes I knew from my grandmother's cooking: sticky rice, laap, tam mak hoong, grilled fish, fresh herbs. The flavors were identical. Of course they were.

I couldn't join the conversations in Lao or Thai, but I could follow them. I understood more than I could speak — a strange position to be in, like watching a film with the subtitles almost right. My dad translated the important things. The rest I read in people's faces. There was a lot of catching up. A lot of laughter. Some tears at the edges of things. Decades of distance compressed into a week of meals.

My grandmother Soumaly had been the one who kept the food alive in our family — the recipes, the techniques, the specific way certain dishes were supposed to taste. She wasn't there with us in Nakhon Phanom. But eating at that table with her siblings, I understood exactly where she had learned it. The food was a direct line to her.

"The flavors were identical to my grandmother's cooking. Of course they were. She had learned them at this same table, in this same town."

Outdoor meal · Nakhon Phanom

Food prepared fresh every day — familiar flavors from my grandmother's kitchen, traced back to their origin

Stop 05 · The Last Morning

Before the Road East

On our last full day in Nakhon Phanom, my dad and I went back to the Mekong one more time. We walked out onto the sandbar near the bank — wide and pale in the dry-season sun, the river running low and quiet beside us. One of his uncles came with us, the three of us standing there not saying much.

I thought about my grandmother the whole time. She had grown up two blocks from this river and left it behind. She had raised a family in Alabama — in Opelika, then Montgomery — taught us to fish on lakes that looked nothing like the Mekong, made food from recipes she carried in her memory, poured her love into grandchildren who didn't fully know where that love came from. I knew now. I was standing in it.

The next morning we loaded into a car and drove east. The flat Isan plain stretched out on either side of the road — rice fields, palm trees, the occasional wat. The Mekong reappeared at the border. On the other side was Savannakhet, Laos. My grandfather's hometown. My dad's childhood. Another chapter in the same story.

Thailand had been a week. It felt like much longer.

Family gathering · Nakhon Phanom, February 2008

Before we left, we took a photo in front of the house. My dad's aunts and uncles, cousins, us. Everyone standing together in the morning light. I've thought about that photo a lot since then — who is in it, what it took for all of us to end up alive and in the same frame at the same time. The war. The river. The refugee camp. A family from Dadeville, Alabama called the Olivers who agreed to sponsor strangers from the other side of the world.

My family settled in Opelika, then Montgomery. I was born there. Years later I'd end up at Auburn for college — twenty minutes from where the family first landed — without fully registering what that meant at the time. It takes a trip like this one to start connecting those dots.

My grandmother never made it back to Nakhon Phanom. I'm glad I went for her.

About Soumaly

My grandmother grew up in Nakhon Phanom on the banks of the Mekong. Her family fled into Thailand during the Vietnam War era and were reunited at a refugee camp before immigrating to the United States. She brought her food, her warmth, and her way of loving with her. This trip was, in some ways, a visit to the origin of all of it.
